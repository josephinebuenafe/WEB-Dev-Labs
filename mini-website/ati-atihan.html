History and Significance

The Ati-Atihan Festival is celebrated every January in Kalibo, Aklan, and is widely known as the "Mother of All Philippine Festivals." It traces its roots to the 13th century when the native Aetas (or Atis) and Malay settlers lived peacefully together. Over the centuries, it evolved into a vibrant celebration honoring the Santo Niño (Child Jesus), blending indigenous traditions with Christian devotion.

During the festivities, participants paint their faces with black soot, wear colorful tribal costumes, and dance to the rhythmic beat of drums while chanting "Hala Bira!" The event highlights unity, cultural pride, and spiritual faith. Today, the Ati-Atihan not only attracts local devotees but also draws tourists worldwide who come to witness its lively street parades, religious processions, and cultural exhibitions.

Festival Highlights

  • Street dancing with drumbeats and chants of “Hala Bira!”
  • Tribal-inspired costumes and colorful parades
  • Religious procession in honor of the Santo Niño
  • Concerts, food fairs, and cultural showcases

Frequently Asked Questions

When is the Ati-Atihan Festival celebrated?

The festival is held annually every third week of January in Kalibo, Aklan.

What does "Ati-Atihan" mean?

"Ati-Atihan" means "to be like Atis," referring to the indigenous people of the Philippines.

Can tourists join the street dancing?

Yes! Visitors are welcome to join the fun and dance alongside performers in the parade.
