History and Significance

The Moriones Festival is celebrated during Holy Week on the island of Marinduque. It dates back to the 1800s when Spanish friars introduced a Lenten practice of dramatizing the story of **Longinus**, the Roman centurion who pierced the side of Jesus Christ during the Crucifixion. According to tradition, Longinus was converted to Christianity and martyred by his fellow soldiers.

Participants, known as moriones, wear wooden masks, armor, and Roman centurion costumes. They roam the streets reenacting the story of Longinus, often surprising or playfully scaring onlookers. The festival blends religion, theatre, and folk creativity, making it one of the most unique Holy Week observances in the Philippines.

Today, the Moriones Festival attracts thousands of pilgrims and tourists who join in the religious rituals, watch the dramatic street performances, and reflect on the deeper meaning of faith and sacrifice during Lent.

Festival Highlights

  • Street reenactments of the story of Longinus
  • Participants in Roman centurion masks and costumes
  • Passion plays and Holy Week processions
  • Community prayers, Lenten rituals, and reflections

Frequently Asked Questions

When is the Moriones Festival celebrated?

The festival is held during Holy Week, especially on Good Friday, in Marinduque.

What does “Moriones” mean?

“Moriones” refers to the masks and costumes of Roman centurions worn by festival participants.

Is the Moriones Festival religious or cultural?

It is both — deeply rooted in Catholic traditions but also a showcase of local artistry and community culture.
